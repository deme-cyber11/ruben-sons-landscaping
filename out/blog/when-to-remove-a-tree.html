When to Remove a Tree: 7 Warning Signs Every Homeowner Should Know

Trees add beauty and value to your property, but a damaged or dying tree can become a serious hazard. Here's how to know when it's time to call a professional.

As a homeowner in the DMV area, your trees are valuable assets. They provide shade, improve air quality, and can increase your property value by up to 15%. But trees don't live forever, and a compromised tree can cause thousands of dollars in damage—or worse, injure someone.

After 15+ years of tree care in Maryland, Virginia, and DC, we've seen it all. Here are the seven warning signs that indicate a tree may need to be removed.

1. The Trunk Shows Signs of Decay

A healthy tree has solid, intact bark. Watch for these trunk warning signs:

  • Large cavities or holes in the trunk
  • Soft, crumbly wood when you press on it
  • Bark falling off in large sections (not normal shedding)
  • Vertical cracks running down the trunk
  • Mushrooms or fungi growing at the base

Fungi at the base often indicate root rot, which can cause sudden failure even in trees that look healthy above ground.

2. The Tree Is Leaning Significantly

Some trees naturally grow at an angle, but a sudden lean is cause for concern. If you notice:

  • The tree has shifted from its original position
  • Soil is heaving up on one side of the base
  • Exposed roots on the opposite side of the lean
  • The lean has increased over time

A leaning tree with root damage can fall without warning. This is especially dangerous after storms when soil is saturated.

3. Dead Branches in the Crown

Dead branches (sometimes called "widow makers") are one of the most common hazards. A few dead twigs are normal, but watch for:

  • Large dead branches over 3 inches in diameter
  • More than 25% of the crown appears dead
  • Dead branches on one side only (indicates root or trunk damage on that side)
  • Branches dying from the top down (often indicates root problems)

Sometimes aggressive pruning can save a tree with dead branches. But if the crown is more than 50% dead, removal is usually the safest option.

4. Root Problems Are Visible

Tree roots extend far beyond the trunk—often 2-3 times the width of the crown. Root damage from construction, soil compaction, or disease can kill a tree. Warning signs include:

  • Mushrooms growing in a ring around the tree
  • Recent construction or trenching near the tree
  • Soil heaving or cracking around the base
  • Severed or damaged visible roots
  • The tree is declining after nearby landscaping changes

5. Storm Damage Has Compromised Structure

The DMV gets its share of severe weather. After a storm, inspect your trees for:

  • Split trunks (especially V-shaped unions)
  • Large torn branches still hanging in the tree
  • Lightning strikes (can kill a tree over time)
  • More than 50% crown loss

A tree can sometimes recover from storm damage with proper care, but structural damage often worsens over time.

6. The Tree Is Too Close to Structures

Sometimes a healthy tree simply isn't in the right place. Consider removal if:

  • Roots are damaging your foundation, driveway, or pipes
  • Branches are hitting your roof or power lines
  • The tree blocks essential sunlight to your home or garden
  • It's causing drainage problems

In these cases, it's often better to remove the tree and plant a more appropriate species elsewhere.

7. Disease or Pest Infestation

The Mid-Atlantic region has seen several tree diseases and pest outbreaks. Signs of serious problems include:

  • Emerald Ash Borer: D-shaped exit holes, crown dieback in ash trees
  • Oak Wilt: Rapid browning of leaves starting at the edges
  • Dutch Elm Disease: Yellowing and wilting of leaves on one branch, then spreading
  • Bacterial Leaf Scorch: Brown, scorched-looking leaves with yellow borders

Some diseases can spread to nearby trees. In these cases, prompt removal protects the rest of your landscape.

What to Do Next

If you've noticed any of these warning signs, don't wait. A compromised tree can fail at any time, especially during storms. Here's what we recommend:

  1. Don't go under the tree if you suspect it's hazardous
  2. Get a professional assessment from a certified arborist
  3. Get multiple quotes if removal is recommended
  4. Check for permits (Montgomery County, Fairfax County, and others require permits for large trees)
  5. Consider stump removal to prevent regrowth and pest problems
